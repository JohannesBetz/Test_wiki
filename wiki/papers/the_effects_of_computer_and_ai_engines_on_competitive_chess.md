# The Effects of Computer and AI Engines on Competitive Chess

> The Effects of Computer and AI Engines on Competitive Chess | Journal of Student Research | Ethan Modi and Kymberly Acuna

## Summary

This paper examines how computer and AI chess engines have changed competitive chess. Its main claim is that engines such as Deep Blue, Stockfish, and AlphaZero have strengthened analysis and raised the level of competition, but they have also shifted the game toward deeper preparation, heavier engine dependence, and possible erosion of human originality.

The paper is not about autonomous systems in the embodied sense. In this vault, it is useful as an adjacent example of how AI systems alter expert performance, training habits, and evaluation standards in a strategic competitive domain.

## Argument

The paper presents a balanced position rather than a simple celebration of machine superiority.

Its basic line is:

- engines improve analysis quality and make preparation much stronger,
- engines can expand the reachable space of ideas and attract wider attention to the game,
- but overreliance on engine outputs can reduce creative independence and push players toward memorization-heavy competition.

The endpoint is a hybrid recommendation: strong chess in the AI era should use engines as tools, not as replacements for human strategic thought.

## Notes / Critique

The best part of the paper is its balanced framing. It does not treat AI as purely corrosive or purely liberating; it treats engines as tools that both expand and distort expert competition.

Its main limitation is that it is more reflective than empirical. It synthesizes a recognizable story about engine-era chess, but it is not a controlled technical evaluation of human-engine interaction.
