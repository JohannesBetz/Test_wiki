# Do Autonomous Vehicles Outperform Latest-Generation Human-Driven Vehicles? A Comparison to Waymo's Auto Liability Insurance Claims at 25 Million Miles

> Do Autonomous Vehicles Outperform Latest-Generation Human-Driven Vehicles? A Comparison to Waymo's Auto Liability Insurance Claims at 25 Million Miles | Traffic Injury Prevention

## Summary

This paper compares Waymo's autonomous-vehicle safety outcomes against latest-generation human-driven vehicles using auto liability insurance claims over 25 million autonomous miles.

For this vault, the paper is important because it provides a real-world, deployment-scale human-versus-autonomy comparison in road driving. That makes it a useful counterpoint to the vault's motorsport and simulator-focused comparisons, where the main question is performance at the limit rather than safety at scale.

## Core Idea

The main contribution is evaluative rather than algorithmic. The paper asks whether autonomous vehicles appear to outperform human-driven vehicles when measured through large-scale real-world claims outcomes.

That matters because AV discourse often swings between:

- highly controlled benchmark claims,
- spectacular edge-case demonstrations,
- and very broad public statements about safety.

A claims-based comparison is one of the few ways to tie the discussion back to operational evidence.

## Why It Matters

This paper broadens the vault's comparison branch in a useful way.

Compared with [[human_vs_machine_in_the_future_of_motorsport_can_autonomous_vehicles_compete]], the question here is not whether machines can compete meaningfully in a high-performance domain, but whether they already look safer than humans in large-scale road deployment.

Compared with [[on_the_human_machine_gap_in_car_racing_a_comparative_analysis_of_machine_performance_against_human_drivers]], the gap being measured is not racing competence but safety-relevant operational outcome.

That makes the paper a useful anchor for the road-autonomy side of human-versus-machine evaluation.

## Notes / Critique

The strongest contribution is scale and framing. The paper helps anchor AV safety discussion in operational evidence rather than only in architecture claims or isolated demonstrations.

Its main limitation for this vault is domain distance. It is not about high-dynamic autonomy or motorsport-style control, so its value is more comparative and evaluative than directly technical.
