# AI & Human Co-Improvement for Safer Co-Superintelligence

> AI & Human Co-Improvement for Safer Co-Superintelligence | arXiv 2025 | Jason Weston and Jakob Foerster

## Summary

This paper argues that as AI systems become more capable, safety may depend not only on alignment constraints or external oversight, but on a process of `co-improvement` between humans and AI.

For this vault, that makes the paper less about narrow autonomy methods and more about the long-range architecture of intelligence:

**how should increasingly capable AI systems and human agents evolve together so that capability growth remains cooperative rather than destabilizing?**

## Method

This is a strategic and conceptual paper, not a robotics benchmark or a direct control method.

Its key move is to shift the safety question away from a simple `control the AI` framing and toward a richer `co-develop humans and AI together` framing.

That makes it a natural fit for the vault's [[human_ai_collaboration]] branch, but at a much larger scale than ordinary assistant-style collaboration. It is really about the future design of high-capability hybrid intelligence.

## Key Results

The strongest contribution is conceptual.

The paper suggests that safer high-capability AI may require:

- better human-AI complementarity,
- mutual improvement rather than one-sided optimization,
- and a systems view in which human capability and AI capability are not treated as separable tracks.

That makes it a useful counterpart to more capability-centered foundation-model papers. It reminds the vault that when intelligence scales, the real question may no longer be only `what can the model do?` but also `what kind of human-AI regime does the model create?`

## Notes / Critique

The paper is useful because it insists that safety is not only a matter of constraining a system after the fact. It may also be a matter of designing the human-AI relation correctly from the start.

The main caveat is concreteness. The co-improvement framing is strategically appealing, but it remains harder to operationalize than benchmark-oriented capability or alignment proposals.
