# Human- versus Artificial Intelligence

> Human- versus Artificial Intelligence | Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence | J. E. (Hans) Korteling, G. C. van de Boer-Visschedijk, R. A. M. Blankendaal, R. C. Boonekamp, and A. R. Eikelboom

## Summary

This paper compares human intelligence and artificial intelligence and argues that they should not be treated as if one were simply a copy target for the other. Instead of assuming that human-like intelligence is the gold standard, it proposes a broader view in which AI may have fundamentally different strengths, constraints, and forms of generality.

For this vault, the paper matters because it helps explain why many autonomy questions are really questions about complementarity: when should AI replace human judgment, when should it support it, and when do the two have fundamentally different cognitive advantages?

## Core Idea

The paper centers three claims:

- both human and artificial intelligence are constrained in different ways,
- human intelligence is only one possible form of general intelligence,
- and the most important near-term impact may come from integrated narrow or hybrid AI systems rather than one singular human-copying AGI.

This leads to a practical collaboration question: how should humans and AI systems be combined so that each compensates for the limitations of the other?

## Notes / Critique

The strongest contribution is its anti-anthropomorphic framing. It helps move the discussion away from "is AI human-like yet?" toward "what kind of intelligence is this, what are its constraints, and how should it be combined with human judgment?"

Its main limitation for this vault is practical distance. It offers a useful conceptual map, but not a direct recipe for how to engineer successful human-AI collaboration in specific autonomous systems.
