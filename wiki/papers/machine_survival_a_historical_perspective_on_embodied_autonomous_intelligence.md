# Machine Survival: A Historical Perspective on Embodied Autonomous Intelligence

> Machine Survival: A Historical Perspective on Embodied Autonomous Intelligence | IOP Conference Series: Materials Science and Engineering, 2026 | Yoshida | DOI: 10.1088/1757-899X/1343/1/012011

## Summary

This paper reviews the history of [[embodied_autonomous_intelligence]] through the lens of survival. Its main argument is that one important research tradition in robotics and artificial agents does not treat intelligence as a bag of separate skills, but as a holistic property of an embodied individual that must maintain itself over time.

The review emphasizes homeostasis as the recurring organizing principle. In this view, intelligent behavior is not only about completing externally assigned tasks. It is also about preserving suitable internal bodily states, making survival itself a primitive objective.

## Core Idea

The paper contrasts function-specific AI and robotics work with a more integrated line of embodied research. The modular tradition builds planning systems, visual recognition models, emotional systems, or language models separately. The embodied-autonomy tradition instead asks how coherent adaptive behavior emerges when sensing, acting, and internal regulation are coupled in one agent.

This shifts the design target. Rather than optimizing explicit external goals alone, the agent can be understood as a system whose behavior is organized around continued viability.

## Key Themes

The review traces this perspective from classical cybernetics to more recent embodied and biologically inspired AI work. It highlights:

- Survival as a defining trait of animal-like intelligence.
- Homeostasis as a mechanism for grounding behavior in internal need.
- Embodiment as a constraint that makes intelligence inseparable from body and environment.
- The possibility that future autonomous agents may benefit from viability-oriented internal objectives rather than only externally specified tasks.

The paper also discusses future directions and grand challenges for embodied autonomous intelligence in light of current AI progress.

## Relevance to This Vault

This paper is not about autonomous racing directly, but it is useful as conceptual scaffolding. Racing systems are usually built around explicit performance objectives such as lap time, safety, and constraint satisfaction. This review asks what changes when autonomy is grounded first in internal regulation and survival.

That makes it a useful companion to [[cognitive_navigation_review]] and to the broader stack summarized in [[autonomous_vehicles_on_the_edge]], especially when thinking about robustness, adaptation, and embodied agency.

## Notes / Critique

The strongest contribution is conceptual integration. The paper gives a clean historical story for why homeostasis and embodiment matter in autonomy research.

Its main limitation for this vault is practical distance. It offers framing rather than direct algorithms, benchmarks, or implementation guidance for racing systems.
