# Autonomous Learning

## Definition

Autonomous learning is the idea that an intelligent system should be able to acquire, refine, and reorganize competence through its own ongoing interaction, observation, and internal adaptation rather than depending mainly on fixed human-curated supervision.

In this vault, the term matters as a stricter standard than ordinary training success. A system may optimize well on data or benchmarks without yet being a genuinely autonomous learner.

## Key Framing

[[why_ai_systems_dont_learn_and_what_to_do_about_it_lessons_on_autonomous_learning_from_cognitive_science]] gives the clearest current framing in the vault.

It argues that present-day AI systems often fall short of autonomous learning in a deeper sense, and that cognitive science offers lessons about what is missing.

This topic therefore sits at an important junction:

- [[era_of_experience]] says future systems must learn from grounded experience,
- [[foundation_models_for_intelligent_decision_making]] shows how much present capability still depends on pretrained priors,
- and autonomous learning asks whether those systems can actually become self-improving learners rather than only powerful pattern followers.

## Relationship to AGI, Experience, and Embodiment

This topic sits naturally near [[artificial_general_intelligence]], [[era_of_experience]], and [[embodied_autonomous_intelligence]].

The AGI branch raises the question of whether broad competence is enough.

The experience branch raises the question of whether grounded interaction is the necessary substrate.

The embodiment branch raises the question of whether learning requires a body-environment loop rather than only symbolic optimization.

Autonomous learning is where those three questions meet.

## Open Problems

Open problems include:

- what architectural ingredients autonomous learning actually requires,
- how to distinguish true autonomous learning from large-scale benchmark optimization,
- how to make self-directed learning safe and data-efficient,
- and how to connect cognitive-science insights to concrete autonomy systems rather than leaving them as high-level critique alone.
