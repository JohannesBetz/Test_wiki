## Objective

RECOVER.ME aims to give robots a more human-like ability to understand and recover from severe but rectifiable hardware malfunctions.

The core hypothesis, as stated on the CORDIS fact sheet, is that robots equipped with human-like metacognitive awareness and regulatory abilities can handle certain hardware faults by themselves rather than being taken fully out of operation and troubleshot manually.

The motivating application is robotic space exploration. The project argues that future remote and infrastructure-building robots will need much stronger autonomous failure handling, because hardware faults in dangerous distant environments cannot always wait for expert human intervention.

## Relevance to the Vault

This grant is not part of the autonomous-racing branch directly, but it is highly relevant to the vault's broader interests in embodied autonomy, resilience, and systems that must continue operating under severe real-world uncertainty.

Its main contribution is conceptual:

- it treats failure handling as part of intelligence rather than as an external maintenance activity,
- it frames metacognition as a route to resilience,
- and it pushes the vault toward a richer notion of autonomy where staying operational under internal malfunction matters as much as optimizing nominal behavior.

## Notes

The important thing about RECOVER.ME for this vault is that it broadens the meaning of autonomy.

Many pages in this wiki focus on speed, agility, and control near external physical limits. RECOVER.ME adds a different but complementary limit case: what happens when the robot itself is partially broken, and the hard problem becomes understanding and regulating failure rather than only tracking, planning, or racing fast.

That makes it a valuable anchor for future work on resilient embodied systems, especially if the vault later grows a stronger space robotics or failure-recovery branch.
